1 INTRODUCTION
A growing literature examines how corporate social responsibility (CSR) becomes strategically consequential through external evaluation. Stakeholders and CSR research has long argued that firms can create value by managing relationships with stakeholders, reducing social and environmental conflict, and building reservoirs of goodwill that protect firms when adverse events occur (Barnett, 2007; Barnett & Salomon, 2012; Freeman, 1984; Godfrey et al., 2009; McWilliams & Siegel, 2001). Subsequent work has extended this logic by showing that capital-market actors and evaluative intermediaries participate in the construction of CSR as a strategic signal. Ratings and rankings convert diffuse social and environmental information into visible comparisons, and firms can respond to unfavourable ratings by improving environmental practices when ratings expose them to stakeholder, regulatory, or reputational pressure (Chatterji & Toffel, 2010). Institutional investors also shape this evaluative environment because they increasingly treat environmental and social performance as financially and socially relevant, engage portfolio firms on ESG concerns, and influence corporate environmental and social practices across countries (Dimson et al., 2015; Dyck et al., 2019; Ilhan et al., 2023). Sovereign wealth funds (SWFs) occupy a distinctive position in this literature conversation. They are large state-owned institutional investors whose equity holdings can affect market interpretations of target firms, and prior research shows that their investments generate valuation consequences that vary with fund transparency, governance, political involvement, and investment orientation (Bernstein et al., 2013; Bortolotti et al., 2015; Dewenter et al., 2010; Kotter & Lel, 2011). Taken together, this stream of work has established that CSR is a socially evaluated signal, that institutional investors can transmit and enforce expectations about responsible corporate conduct, and that SWFs are consequential but heterogeneous actors in global capital markets.
However, although this literature has substantially advanced the understanding of how responsible investors may select and influence firms (e.g., xxx), it has paid less attention to the possibility that visible responsible ownership can also change the marginal value of further CSR improvement (xxx). The prevailing investor-influence logic often treats responsible investors as sources of continuing pressure. This logic is useful because many investors use engagement, voting, screening, and portfolio allocation to encourage stronger environmental and social performance (Dimson et al., 2015; Dyck et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2020). Yet the same public association can also operate through a certification logic. Prior research on certification and endorsement shoes that prominent external affiliates can reduce uncertainty about the focal organization by transferring status, visibility, or credibility to it (Megginson & Weiss, 1991; Stuart et al., 1999; Pollock & Gulati, 2007). If this logic applies to socially responsible SWFs (SRSWFs), their holdings may be interpreted by managers and external audiences as evidence that the focal firm has passed a meaningful responsibility screen. This implication is not well developed in existing CSR and sustainable-finance research. Studies on CSR ratings and ESF information shoe that evaluative intermediaries influence managerial attention and firm responses, especially when poor ratings expose firms to reputational pressure (Chatterji & Toffel, 2010). Studies on social ratings also show that firms may respond differently to favourable evaluations, including cases in which positive CSR-related evaluations reduce subsequent prosocial activity when those evaluations alter the social meaning of further giving (Lewis & Carlos, 2002). At the same time, research on ESG-rating divergence shows that ratings often disagree because agencies differ in what they measure, how they measure it, and how they weigh indicators (xxx); such divergence complicates interpretation and can weaken incentives to improve social- and environment-related performance (Chatterji et al., 2016; Berg et al., 2022; Christensen et al., 2022). These findings create an unresolved issue. If CSR operates through external evaluation, and if responsible investors can certify the focal firm by visibly holding its shares, then SRSWFs ownership may decrease the perceived necessity of continued CSR escalation. The question is therefore not only whether responsible investors improve CSR. The question is when their public endorsement may induce a plateau in subsequent CSR improvement by making the firm’s existing CSR level appear sufficiently acceptable.
The purpose of this paper is to explain how SRSWF ownership affects subsequent CSR improvement in portfolio firms. This paper argues that SRSWFs can function as certifying signallers. Because such funds combine public ownership, reputational exposure, long investment horizons, and explicit responsible-investment commitments, their visible equity holdings can be interpreted as a selection signal that the focal firm has satisfied a consequential external evaluator. This certification does not imply that the firm has achieved an optimal or complete level of CSR performance. It implies that the managers and external audiences may infer that the firm’s current CSR position has crossed a socially acceptable threshold for a prominent responsible investor. This inference reduces the marginal pressure to continue improving CSR, because further improvement required managerial attention, organizational coordination, and resource commitments, while the certification signal already supplies a degree of external validation. The paper therefore predicts a certification-induced plateau: firms held by SRSWFs will exhibit lower subsequent CSR improvement than comparable firms that lack such holdings. The paper also argues that this effect depends on the clarity of the broader evaluative environment. When CSR or ESG ratings are relatively convergent, the SRSWF holding and their-party ratings mutually reinforce the inference that the firm’s CSR performance is acceptable. When ratings diverge, the certification signal becomes less settled because external audiences receive competing evaluations of the firm’s CSR standing. Evaluative ambiguity should therefore weaken the negative relationship between SRSWF holding and subsequent CSR improvement. This argument contributes to research on CSR by shifting attention from the level of responsible conduct to marginal dynamics of CSR improvement after external validation. It contributes to research on institutional investors by showing that responsible ownership may operate through certifications as well as monitoring or engagement. It also contributes to research on SWFs by specifying why heterogeneity among funds matters for the social meaning of their holdings, rather than treating SWF ownership as a uniform capital-market event. 
2 THEORY AND HYPOTHESIS
2.1 Corporate Social Responsibility as a Socially Evaluated Signal
The performance of a firm’s corporate social responsibility (CSR) is not merely a set of internal policies, practices, and outcomes, but also as a signal that becomes meaningful through external evaluation. Signalling theory argues that, when information is unevenly distributed, actors can reduce uncertainty by sending observable signals about less observable qualities, and receivers use these signals to update their judgements (Spence, 1973; Connelly et al., 2011). In the CSR context, the qualities being inferred are not limited to the immediate substance of social and environmental activities. CSR-related actions, CSR disclosures, and their-party CSR assessments may also inform external audiences about a firm’s stakeholder orientation, governance quality, social and regulatory risk exposure, and capacity for long-term reliability. CSR is therefore both a domain of corporate conduct and an object of social interpretation.
This distinction matters because many CSR-relevant attributes are difficult for external audiences to observe directly, comprehensively, and in real time. Stakeholders usually cannot fully verify how a firm manages environmental externalities, protects employee rights and welfare, treat suppliers and customers, engages with local communities, or governs managerial discretion. They therefore rely on indirect informational devices, including corporate reports, media coverage, civil society scrutiny, their-party ratings, and the visible actions of influential investors. Prior research suggests that stronger CSR performance can improve stakeholder relations and generate a form of reputational insurance, because favourable prior evaluations may increase interpretive latitude and stakeholder tolerance when firms face adverse events (Barnett, 2007; Godfrey et al., 2009). The strategic value of CSR thus lies not only in the focal firm’s concrete social responsibility practices, but also in the evaluations that external audiences construct from those practices. 
Third-party evaluators occupy a central position in this process. Because CSR activities are multidimensional, unevenly disclosed, and difficult to compare across firms, rating agencies translate heterogeneous corporate actions into relatively standardised ratings, rankings, and classificatory judgements. These evaluative outputs do more than summarize information. They also shape the signalling environment by defining what counts as responsible corporate conduct, by comparing firms against peers, and by making evaluative judgements visible to managers, investors, and other stakeholders. Firms may respond to such external assessments, especially when unfavourable ratings threaten their perceived legitimacy and increase pressure to improve their environmental and social performance (Chatterji & Toffel, 2010). CSR rating agencies are therefore not merely passive recorders of corporate behaviour. They are active intermediaries that help determine how CSR signals are produced, interpreted, and circulated.
However, CSR signals generated through evaluative intermediaries are not always clear or consistent. Ratings issued by different agencies can vary substantially and often do not converge (Chatterji et al., 2016). Such divergence reflects differences in evaluation scope, measurement methods, indicator selection, and weighing schemes (Berg et al., 2022). Moreover, greater disclosure does not necessarily reduce disagreement among evaluators. In some cases, it may even increase divergence, especially when disclosure expands the amount of outcome-related information that agencies interpret differently (Christensen et al., 2022). This means that CSR evaluation systems perform two functions at the same time. They reduce information asymmetry by making firms more comparable, but they may also introduce ambiguity because different evaluators attach different meanings to similar CSR-related evidence. 
We therefore conceptualize CSR as a socially evaluated signal with three connected layers. First, a firm’s CSR actions and disclosures provide observable cues about its social orientation and governance quality. Second, CSR rating agencies and other evaluative intermediaries interpret these cues and convert them into comparable assessments. Third, salient market actors, including institutional investors and sovereign wealth funds (SWFs), may incorporate these assessments into broader judgments about whether to invest in, hold, engage with, or divest from focal firms. These investment decisions should not be treated as mechanically determined by CSR ratings, because investors typically rely on multiple financial, strategic, political, and institutional considerations. Yet once such actors publicly allocate, withhold, or withdraw capital, their actions can become secondary signals that further influence how other audiences interpret the focal firm. This layered signalling environment provides the theoretical basis for examining the role of socially responsible SWFs in shaping the market consequences of CSR.
2.2 Socially Responsible SWFs as Certifying Signallers
Sovereign wealth funds (SWFs) are significant institutional investors that are owned or controlled by the state and conduct global asset allocation in the form of investment portfolios. Their investments influence the market valuation of and external cognition to the focal firms. Announcements of investing from SWFs are normally accompanied by observable market reactions (Dewenter et al., 2010); and Kotter & Lel (2011) further elucidates that heterogeneous nature of the funds, including strategies of investments and various degrees of transparency, relates to such reactions differently. Meanwhile, SWFs differ significantly in structures of governance and levels of political involvement, so they should not be perceived as homogenous investor (Bernstein et al., 2013). 
Among these heterogeneities, we focus on socially responsible SWFs (SRSWFs) rather than SWFs in general. SWFs like the Government Pension Fund Global (GPFP), New Zealand Super Fund (NZSF), and Ireland Strategic Investment Fund (ISIF) have clear policies in responsible investment, high levels of transparency, and a strong orientation towards long-term investment. Norges Bank Investment Management (NBIM), the manager of GPFP, publicly emphasizes principles of responsible investment, opening dialogues with investee firms, and practices of voting; NZSF regularly discloses its equity holdings, direct investments and external investment authorizations; ISIF also clearly links responsible investment with the protection of long-term value and reputation. In addition, each of them are one of the earliest funds adopting negative screening which publicly and proactively screens out equities violating their own responsible investment conduct. The public institutional arrangements of the aforementioned funds make it easier for the market to identify and interpret their shareholding behaviour as socially responsible investment choice.
SRSWFs can be identified as certifying signallers. Certifying logic has a solid foundation in organizational research and financial research. Endorsement from prominent affiliations usually enhances the focal firm’s visibility by providing strong signals of legitimacy (Pollock & Gulati, 2007). Venture capitals offer certification for investee firms, reducing the doubts of external investors about the quality of the firms in initial public offerings (IPOs) (Megginson & Weiss, 1991). Similarly, establishing connections with high-status organizations enhances the external reputation of young enterprises, as external audiences will view the choice of high-status partners as indirect recognition of the quality of the enterprise (Stuart et al., 1999). These studies indicate that public associations of high-reputation external actors can serve as a certification signal.
SRSWFs have similar certification functions. They are not ordinary external onlookers, but bear capital risks, reputation risks and public accountability pressures with observable investment portfolios. Their principle of responsible investment also makes it easier for external audiences to understand their shareholding behaviour as approval after screening. For business managers, holding shares in SRSWFs means that a highly visible, responsible and long-term-oriented capital holder is willing to entre or continue to hold the company’s stocks; and for external stakeholders, SRSWFs’ shareholding means that the enterprise is not excluded from the scope of responsible investment by such investors at least by certain observable criteria. As a result, SRSWF holdings connect the CSR signals of enterprises themselves, the evaluation signals of rating agencies, and the selection signal of investors. 
2.3 SRSWF Endorsement and Subsequent CSR Improvement
In the field of CSR evaluation, the continuous improvement of CSR performance of a firm usually demonstrates strategic rationality. Firms sustain legitimacy, respond to pressures from stakeholders, reduce reputational risks, and strengthen long-term relational capitals through improving their CSR performance. The benefit of higher CSR performance is not necessarily linear, and it depends on whether focal firms can convert such performance into support and trust from stakeholders and the social capitals (Bernett, 2007; Godfrey et al., 2009). This means that a firm’s investment in CSR will be influenced by the judgement of marginal returns. When external pressure is high, the threat to legitimacy is strong, or the evaluation is at a disadvantage, firms are more likely to continue to enhance CSR performance. When external signals indicate that its level of social responsibility has been recognised, the marginal impetus to continue significantly improving CSR will decline. 
SRSWF shareholdings will change this marginal driving force through certifying logic. When a SRSWF publicly holds an equity, firm managers will receive a positive selection signal from a highly visible responsible investor. It does not mean that the current CSR level of the focal firm is perfect; rather, it reduces managers’ uncertainty about whether the current CSR level is sufficient. As this type of SWF explicitly adheres to the principle of responsible investment and implement certain screening process of investment, managers can reasonably infer that the current CSR status of the firm has at least reached a level acceptable to prominent responsible investors. 
External stakeholders handle this signal in a similar way. When signals emitted both by CSR rating agencies and through shareholding from SRSWFs superimpose, external audiences are more likely to form a judgement that the firm has been recognised by reputable responsible investors. Studies on certification reveal that high-status external connections can changes the external audience’s inference about the quality of a firm, because the audience will understand the selection of high-reputation actors as a kind of screening result (Stuart et al., 1999; Megginson & Weiss, 1991). In our context, SRSWF shareholding provides an additional external endorsement for CSR signals of firms.
This endorsement will reduce the marginal necessity for subsequent CSR improvements of firms. Managers are confronted with constraints of resource allocation. CSR improvements require managerial attention, organizational coordination, substantive investments, and other organizational resources that can be scarce. When SRSWF shareholdings signify that the investee firm has reached acceptable level of CSR performance, providing endorsements and alleviating doubts on its inadequacy of performing social responsibility from external audiences, the incentive for managers to continue making significant CSR improvements will decline. [Research on legitimacy and subsequent decision on CSR investments shows that such certification/endorsement will induce a decline in firms’ efforts to escalate CSR performance.] Having said that, this mechanism emphasizes the speed of subsequent upgrades or the extent of marginal improvements, rather than the decrease in the level of CSR performance itself. In other words, SRSWF shareholding is more likely to bring firms into a relatively plateau period of CSR improvements, as the certification signal makes it easier for both managers and external audiences to believe that the current CSR performance is already sufficiently acceptable. Therefore, we give our hypothesis:
Hypothesis 1. Firms held by SRSWFs will exhibit lower subsequent CSR improvement than comparable firms not held by SRSWFs.
2.4 Evaluative Ambiguity as a Boundary Condition of the Signalling Environment
 The certification effect signalled from shareholding by SRSWFs relies on qualities of the signalling environment. The degree to which a receiver’s interpretations and judgments are shaped by signals depends on the stability of the linkage between those signals and unobservable underlying qualities (Spence, 1973; Connelly et al., 2011). In the context of CSR, however, this linkage is not always stable. The evaluation of CSR performance itself is usually with multiple dimensions, audiences, and standards. Focuses on different topics and agendas on responsibility among rating agencies could vary, with disparate indicators and weights being applied in the evaluation. Therefore, their ratings do not necessarily converge, potentially resulting in evaluative ambiguity, which is one of the major qualities of the signalling environment in this context.
Evaluative ambiguity is different from solely information insufficiency. The latter is when external audiences are a lack of adequate information to make judgements on actual CSR performance of the firm, whereas the former means different evaluators make discrepant assessments upon the same firm even when information is abundant. Furthermore, information insufficiency can be alleviated by augmenting its amount while ambiguity involves equivocality of explanatory frameworks and the providence of more information has no necessary connection with more consistent level of understanding (Daft & Lengel, 1986; Divergence literature). The divergence in CSR ratings precisely reflects the instability of this evaluative connotation. 
In the context of lower evaluative ambiguity, the certification effect of the signal from SRSWF shareholding becomes stronger. When CSR ratings from different agencies for a given firm are relatively convergent, managers and external stakeholders can more easily align the signal conveyed by SRSWF shareholding with that from the relevant agencies’ CSR ratings. As a result, the signals from rating agencies and those from SRSWF shareholding achieve mutual corroboration. On this basis, firm managers are more likely to perceive that their current CSR performance is recognized by significant external actors, and other external stakeholders are also inclined to accept such recognition. The fact that SRSWF holds shares thus makes it more likely to reduce the marginal incentive for the firm to further improve its CSR.
However, in the context of higher evaluative ambiguity, the certification effect of the signal from SRSWF shareholding recedes. When ratings on a given firm from different agencies diverge, external audiences cannot reach consensus on its CSR performance. At this stage, the signal from SRSWF shareholding remains important, yet it is embedded among divergent—and even conflicting—signals from rating agencies. Based on this, managers find it difficult to determine whether the firm’s CSR level is sufficient. Meanwhile, external stakeholders may also continue to review, as the divergence in signals could still indicate that the firm has deficiencies in certain dimensions. In this scenario, the sufficiency derived from SRSWF shareholding is undermined, thereby attenuating the decline in firms’ incentive to further improve their CSR.
This boundary condition simultaneously incorporates rating agencies into the signalling environment. Rating agencies are the signallers of evaluative signals; while SRSWFs are the ones of certification signals. Firm managers and its external stakeholders are the receivers of both kinds of signals. When rating signals are consistent, SRSWF holdings are more likely to be interpreted as confirmation of the CSR quality of the firm, whereas when rating signals vary to a significant extent, shareholding by SRSWFs is difficult to stabilize the external audiences’ judgement on the quality of the firm’s CSR alone. Therefore, evaluative ambiguity will attenuate the negative influence of SRSWF’s shareholding on the subsequent improvement of the CSR quality of the investee firm. We give our hypothesis:
Hypothesis 2. Evaluative ambiguity weakens the negative relationship between SRSWF holding and subsequent CSR improvement, such that the negative relationship is strong when evaluative ambiguity is low and weaker when evaluative ambiguity is high.
